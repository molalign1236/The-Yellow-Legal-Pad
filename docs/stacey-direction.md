## The business — what it actually is

Financial coaching for people navigating life's biggest transitions.
One-person practice. Small. Personal. Intentionally so.

**What it is not:**
- Not a financial advisory firm
- Not investment management
- No products, no commissions
- *"Think of it as the conversation your financial advisor doesn't have time for"*

**Scale:** small by design. 5–10 clients at a time feels right.
Part-time. Retirement-adjacent. Not replacing career income.

**How clients are found:** word of mouth and warm referrals
primarily. Not chasing broad visibility or social media presence.

**Where sessions happen:** Zoom or phone. Location-independent.
Stacey will be based across Florida, Italy, and eventually SE Asia.

## Voice and tone — Stacey's stated register

**What she actually sounds like:** direct. Warm. Human. Plain
English. No jargon. No corporate speak. No therapy-speak.

### Phrases that feel right (use these where they fit)

- *"Just a conversation"*
- *"No pressure. No pitch."*
- *"Let's look at the real numbers"*
- *"That feeling is exactly why you need this conversation"*
- *"Take your time. Read it with a cup of tea, not between meetings."*

### Phrases that feel wrong (never use)

- "Circle back"
- "Put a pin in it"
- "Leverage your resources"
- "Synergy"
- Anything that sounds like a financial services brochure
- Anything that sounds like a wellness app

**On humor:** present but not performed. Dry. Warm. Never forced.

**On professionalism:** trustworthy without being corporate. The
site should feel like a competent, warm person — not an
institution.

**The word "client":** feels appropriate. Not "customer." Not
"patient."

## Design and atmosphere — Stacey's direction

**The core metaphor:** *"calm, trusted person's kitchen table."*
NOT: consulting firm, fintech product, personal brand, wellness app.

### Visual direction (Stacey's words)

- Soft ivory/cream backgrounds
- Deep navy and warm gold
- Paper and legal pad textures used subtly
- Generous whitespace
- Elegant readable typography
- Nothing that looks like software

### What visual polish feels fake (avoid)

- Stock photos of people pointing at graphs
- Cold blue gradients
- Anything that looks like a fintech dashboard
- Artificial urgency (countdown timers, "only 3 spots left")

**Dark mode:** *matte paper. Not luxury UI.*

**How imperfect should it feel:** human. Readable. Not pixel-perfect
corporate. Small deliberate imperfections are fine if they serve
warmth.

### The bookshelf question — current state

Stacey's earlier brief said: *"Remove it. Return to desk surface
alone. The library reads as credentialed authority — that's not
this practice."*

A later context iteration reframed the bookshelf as legitimate
warmth/dimensionality, not credentialed-authority signaling. The
perceptual audit on 2026-05-12 (see `perceptual-audit-2026-05-12.md`)
verified that the bookshelf dominates desktop visual attention and
recommended **remove entirely** or reduce to **C20** (ghost-pale
architectural pattern).

**Open owner decision.** Both keep-as-is and remove are defensible.
The audit's recommended treatment is removal; the comprehensive
context doc keeps it. Awaiting final owner call before any change.

## Services and client experience

### What the site should make someone feel before reaching out

Safe. Not judged. Like this person understands. That their
situation — however messy — is exactly what this is for.

### Anxieties the site should reduce (Stacey's wording)

- *"I'll be judged for my debt / spending / late start"*
- *"This will try to sell me something"*
- *"I need to have my finances organised before I can talk to someone"*
- *"This is only for people who already have money"*

### Contact flow

- Email first.
- Discovery call via Calendly when the URL is ready.
- The discovery call is **always free. Always low pressure.**

### What should never feel required

- Filling in forms before having a conversation
- Preparing financially before reaching out
- Knowing what package you want before talking

### Current intended flow

1. Discovery call — free, 20–30 minutes, just a conversation
2. Intake form — sent **after** the call, not before
3. Package choice
4. Clarity Session (or whichever package fits)

### What should stay conversational

The first contact. The discovery call. The entire intake process.

## Operational and technical

**Who maintains the site:** Mola builds it. Stacey's technical
comfort is moderate — she can handle content updates if the
interface is simple. Git/GitHub is acceptable for Mola, probably
not for Stacey directly.

**CMS:** worth considering for copy updates Stacey might want to
make herself. Should reduce stress not add it. (Audit pressure:
held off so far — a CMS for a 7-page mostly-stable site is
significant complexity for very few updates per year.)

**Analytics:** not a priority. *Not interested in obsessing over
traffic numbers.*

**Deployment:** Vercel — already decided. Keep it simple.

### Operational burden to avoid

Anything requiring regular maintenance or technical intervention
for routine updates. Anything that makes the site feel like a
product to manage.
